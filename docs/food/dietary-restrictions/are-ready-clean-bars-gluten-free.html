Understanding Gluten and Its Impact on Health

Gluten is a group of proteins found in wheat, barley, rye, and triticale. For most people, gluten is harmless, but for those with celiac disease or gluten sensitivity, consuming gluten can lead to a range of health issues, including digestive problems, fatigue, and even long-term damage to the small intestine. As a result, individuals with these conditions must adhere to a strict gluten-free diet.

Given the potential health risks associated with gluten consumption, it is crucial for those with gluten-related disorders to carefully scrutinize the ingredients and manufacturing processes of the foods they consume. This is where the importance of gluten-free certifications and transparent labeling comes into play.

Are Ready Clean Bars Gluten-Free?

Yes, Ready Clean Bars are gluten-free. The company takes great care to ensure that their products meet the needs of individuals with gluten sensitivities or celiac disease. Here’s a closer look at why Ready Clean Bars are considered a safe option for those avoiding gluten:

Ingredients

Ready Clean Bars are made with a carefully selected list of ingredients that are naturally gluten-free. The primary ingredients include:

  • Protein Blend: Typically sourced from plant-based proteins like pea protein, brown rice protein, or other gluten-free sources.
  • Natural Sweeteners: Such as dates, honey, or stevia, which do not contain gluten.

Manufacturing Process

In addition to using gluten-free ingredients, Ready Clean Bars are manufactured in facilities that are dedicated to producing gluten-free products. This is a critical factor in preventing cross-contamination, which can occur when gluten-free products are processed in the same facilities as those containing gluten. Ready Clean Bars’ commitment to maintaining a gluten-free environment during production further ensures the safety of their products.

Certifications

Ready Clean Bars are certified gluten-free by recognized organizations. This certification involves rigorous testing and verification to ensure that the products contain less than 20 parts per million (ppm) of gluten, which is the threshold established by the FDA for gluten-free labeling. This certification provides an additional layer of assurance for consumers.
